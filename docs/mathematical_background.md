# Mathematical Background: Degree 24 Transitive Groups

This document synthesizes the algebraic foundation required to tackle the SAIR IGP24 competition, focusing on transitive permutation groups, field discriminants, and the Inverse Galois Problem over $\mathbb{Q}$.

## The Inverse Galois Problem (IGP)

The Inverse Galois Problem asks whether every finite group $G$ can be realized as the Galois group of some finite Galois extension over the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$. For a specific degree $d$, the problem reduces to determining if every transitive subgroup of the symmetric group $S_d$ can manifest as the Galois group of an irreducible polynomial of degree $d$ over $\mathbb{Q}$.

### The Degree 24 Frontier

Degree 24 is a critical mathematical boundary. There are exactly **25,000 transitive permutation groups of degree 24** (labeled $24T_1$ through $24T_{25000}$).

- **Solvable Groups**: 24,193 groups.
- **Non-Solvable Groups**: 807 groups.

As of the competition's launch, only a small fraction (fewer than 300) had explicit realizations cataloged in the LMFDB.

## Field Extensions and Splitting Fields

Let $f(x) \in \mathbb{Q}[x]$ be an irreducible monic polynomial of degree 24. 
The roots of $f(x)$ generate a finite extension of $\mathbb{Q}$. The splitting field $K$ is the smallest field extension containing all 24 roots of $f(x)$. The Galois group $\text{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q})$ acts transitively on these roots, embedding it as a transitive subgroup of $S_{24}$.

## Discriminants: Polynomial vs. Number Field

Minimizing the discriminant is the core optimization metric of IGP24.

1. **Polynomial Discriminant**: $\Delta(f)$. This is calculated directly from the roots (or coefficients via the resultant). It often contains large square factors corresponding to the index of the polynomial ring inside the ring of integers.
2. **Number Field Discriminant**: $d_K$. This is the fundamental invariant of the number field generated by a root of $f(x)$. The relation is $\Delta(f) = d_K \cdot i^2$, where $i$ is the index.

> [!NOTE]
> The SAIR IGP24 competition strictly scores based on the absolute number-field discriminant $|d_K|$ (calculated via PARI/GP `nfdisc`). Generating a polynomial with a massive index $i$ is acceptable, provided $|d_K|$ remains small.

## Real Embeddings (Signatures)

A polynomial of degree 24 has 24 complex roots. Because the coefficients are real (integers), non-real roots must occur in conjugate pairs.
The signature is typically denoted by the number of real roots $r$ (where $r \in \{0, 2, 4, \dots, 24\}$). The competition scores unique pairs of $(24T_t, r)$.

## Advanced Structures

- **Resolvents**: Auxiliary polynomials constructed from the roots of $f(x)$. They are central to identifying Galois groups without computing the full splitting field.
- **Hilbert Irreducibility Theorem**: Guarantees that generic polynomials parameterized by $t$ yield the desired Galois group for infinitely many specialized rational integer values of $t$.
- **Largest groups**: $24T_{25000}$ is the full symmetric group $S_{24}$, and the second largest is the alternating group $A_{24}$. Dense random polynomials fall into these, which makes them the least rewarding targets. The value lies in the rare solvable and imprimitive groups.
- **Mathieu groups**: The 5-transitive Mathieu group $M_{24}$ on 24 points is one of the hardest non-solvable targets in this degree. Its cousin $M_{23}$ is the single unresolved case in degree 23 ($23T_5$).
